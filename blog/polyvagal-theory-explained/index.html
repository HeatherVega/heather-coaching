Nervous System

Polyvagal theory, plainly:
why your body isn't broken — it's protecting you

A clear, jargon-free explanation of how your nervous system actually works, and why understanding it changes everything about how you approach healing.

If you've ever wondered why you go blank in an argument, why your body freezes even when your mind wants to move, why you can know something intellectually and still not feel it — polyvagal theory has something important to tell you.

Developed by neuroscientist Stephen Porges, polyvagal theory offers a map of the autonomic nervous system that actually matches lived experience. It explains not just fight-or-flight, but the full range of how your body responds to threat — and crucially, why those responses are intelligent, not broken.

"Your nervous system isn't dysregulated. It's doing exactly what it learned to do to keep you safe."

The three states

Polyvagal theory describes three distinct states your nervous system moves through. The first — and the one we're designed to spend most of our time in — is the ventral vagal state: social engagement, connection, curiosity, calm. This is the state where healing happens, where learning lands, where you feel most like yourself.

When the nervous system detects threat, it moves down a hierarchy. First into sympathetic activation — the mobilization response, fight or flight. Heart rate increases, muscles activate, attention narrows. This is protective. The problem is when the body gets stuck here, or when it perceives threat where there isn't any.

Further down the hierarchy is dorsal vagal collapse — the freeze or shutdown response. Numbness. Disconnection. The sense of not being fully present in your own body. This is the oldest survival response, and it's often the most disorienting to experience.

Why this matters for integration

Psychedelic experiences often surface material that has been held in the nervous system for years — protective adaptations that once made sense and now create constraint. Understanding which state you're in, and having practices that support movement between them, is foundational to integration work. You can't think your way out of a nervous system response. But you can learn to work with it.
