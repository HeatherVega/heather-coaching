5 somatic grounding practices
for after a plant medicine ceremony

Your nervous system just went somewhere big. Here's how to help it land — simple, body-based tools for the days and weeks that follow.

The days after a plant medicine ceremony can feel tender. Wide open. Sometimes disorienting. The veil between what you knew and what you now sense has thinned, and ordinary life can feel both more vivid and strangely unfamiliar. Your nervous system has been through something significant — and it needs time, care, and support to settle.

Somatic grounding practices work by bringing attention back into the body and signaling safety to the nervous system. They're not about shutting down what arose. They're about creating enough regulation that you can stay present with it, rather than dissociating or reaching for old numbing patterns.

These five practices are ones I return to again and again — both in my own work and in guiding others through post-ceremony integration.

"You don't need to process everything at once. You need to be able to stay in your body while it processes you."

1. Orienting

This is the simplest practice and often the most powerful. Slowly turn your head and let your eyes move around the room. Don't rush. Let your gaze rest on each object for a moment — noticing color, shape, texture. This activates the ventral vagal system and sends a signal to the nervous system that you are safe in this moment, in this place.

2. Barefoot contact with the earth

Grass. Soil. Sand. The direct sensation of ground beneath your feet is one of the oldest co-regulatory signals we have. It's not metaphor — physical contact with the earth activates sensory pathways that help regulate the autonomic nervous system. Even five minutes of standing barefoot outside can shift your state meaningfully.

3. Slow exhalation breathing

Extend your exhale to twice the length of your inhale. Breathe in for four counts, out for eight. Repeat for two to three minutes. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic response — the rest-and-digest counterpart to the stress response. This is physiology, not woo. It works.

4. Cold water on face and wrists

The dive reflex — triggered by cold water on the face — dramatically and rapidly slows heart rate and activates the vagus nerve. If you're feeling flooded or overwhelmed in the days after a ceremony, this is a fast way to interrupt the sympathetic activation and bring yourself back to ground.

5. Slow deliberate movement

Not exercise. Movement. A slow walk with attention on the sensation of each step. Gentle stretching with curiosity rather than effort. Swaying. Rocking. The nervous system discharges through movement — gentle, titrated movement that keeps you in the window of tolerance rather than pushing through it.
